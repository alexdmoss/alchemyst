It states that “Stable organometallic complexes are formed when the sum of the neutral metal valence electrons plus the sum of the electrons considered to be donated by the ligand (and taking into account any net charge) equals eighteen”. If it has more d electrons to donate, then the backbonding will be stronger. • Charge on the metal. A more positive metal is less likely to donate its electrons. Thus backbonding has a greater stabilising influence on low oxidation state metals, as they are able to back-bond more effectively (will relinquish their electrons more easily). • Other Ligand present. An electron-withdrawing ligand such as Cp will reduce the degree of back-bonding. Conversely ligands such as alkyls reduce the degree of electron withdrawal. • Coordination Modes. The CO ligand can be terminal, doubly bridging or even triply bridging. This bridging tends to reduce CO bond strength due to greater population of π* CO. The consequence of more back-bonding is a stronger M-C and a weaker C=O bond.
